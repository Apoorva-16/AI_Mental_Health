Title: Mindtrack AI: Early Detection of Mental Health Conditions
Introduction

Mental health has emerged as a key pillar of public health strategy and economic stability in the 21st century. The global burden of mental disorders, particularly depression, anxiety, eating disorders, and schizophrenia, continues to escalate contributing to high disability-adjusted life years (DALYs), decreased workforce productivity, and strained healthcare systems. While mental health awareness is improving, systemic challenges such as fragmented data collection, limited resource allocation, and inconsistent reporting standards persist. Consequently, there is an urgent demand for scalable, data-driven tools that can help identify, predict, and mitigate the impacts of mental illnesses.
Artificial Intelligence (AI) offers unprecedented capabilities to meet this demand. With the ability to parse vast and heterogeneous datasets, detect latent patterns, and forecast health indicators with high precision, AI can significantly augment decision-making processes in mental healthcare. This project presents a novel application of AI through the development of a real-time, predictive dashboard tailored to global mental health trends. By incorporating machine learning models, dynamic visualizations, and user interactivity, the system not only highlights historical trends but also forecasts future developments empowering stakeholders to plan effectively and intervene proactively.
General Overview of Role / Business

The mental health domain encompasses a multidisciplinary set of professionals and stakeholders, including clinicians, epidemiologists, data scientists, policymakers, and public health administrators. Each of these roles intersects with digital tools and data analytics to some extent. However, traditional approaches to mental health data are largely retrospective, static, and disconnected from real-time needs. As a result, decision-makers often struggle to respond to mental health crises with speed and precision.
AI and data science are redefining the scope and efficiency of mental health services. From automated diagnostics and telehealth to population-level forecasting and policy simulation, the integration of advanced analytics is driving innovation across the sector. For example, predictive modeling can help hospitals prepare for spikes in mental health cases, while trend visualization can assist NGOs in targeting awareness campaigns. Businesses and governments alike are increasingly investing in AI-powered mental health technologies to improve outcomes and reduce systemic costs.
In this project, the fusion of AI with interactive data visualization bridges the gap between raw epidemiological data and strategic intervention. The resulting tool allows professionals across various domains to engage with data more intuitively and productively, enabling smarter resource allocation, earlier intervention, and more equitable health outcomes.
Methods and Methodologies

The foundation of this project lies in robust data science practices and machine learning engineering. Several open-access datasets were curated from trusted global sources, covering aspects such as disorder prevalence, geographic distribution, population coverage, and symptomatic breakdowns. Initial preprocessing involved data validation, null value imputation, type normalization, and categorical encoding. Exploratory data analysis (EDA) was conducted using pandas, seaborn, and plotly.express to derive visual and statistical insights into key variables.
Feature engineering focused on identifying reliable predictors of eating disorders, the chosen target variable for forecasting. Four features prevalence rates of depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, and bipolar disorder were selected based on correlation matrices, domain relevance, and data completeness. The modeling pipeline used RandomForestRegressor from sklearn.ensemble due to its robustness against overfitting, interpretability through feature importance scores, and ability to capture non-linear interactions.
To optimize model performance, a standard 80/20 train-test split was implemented, and the dataset was scaled using MinMaxScaler. A FunctionTransformer was applied to log-transform skewed distributions. Hyperparameter tuning was conducted using grid search over parameters such as n_estimators, max_depth, and min_samples_split. Model evaluation metrics included Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), and R^2 score, ensuring comprehensive performance validation. Visual diagnostics such as residual plots and prediction overlays further verified model stability.
Product Overview

The centerpiece of this project is a dynamic, AI-powered mental health dashboard developed using the Streamlit framework and Plotly visualization libraries. The dashboard serves as both an analytical and predictive tool, enabling users to examine historical data and generate forecasts for future mental health trends with an emphasis on eating disorders.
The interface offers a range of features designed for usability and insight generation. Users can select countries of interest and visualize temporal changes in eating disorder prevalence through interactive line graphs. Side-by-side comparisons of different disorders across time and geography offer immediate clarity on regional disparities. A prominent toggle button enables predictive analytics powered by machine learning. Users can choose a target future year between 2024 and 2035, upon which the model generates forecasts based on historical data and selected features.
Additionally, the dashboard includes automatically updated data tables that reflect the latest available statistics for the selected countries and conditions. These features ensure that the tool caters not only to data scientists and researchers, but also to healthcare planners, policy developers, and NGO leaders. Its modular structure supports future expansion with new datasets and functionalities, making it a sustainable solution for mental health monitoring.


Data Collection and Preprocessing
The data collection process for this project was both comprehensive and methodically designed to ensure the integrity and utility of the analysis. Multiple publicly available datasets were sourced from reputable global health organizations, including the World Health Organization (WHO), Our World in Data, and the Global Burden of Disease (GBD) study. These datasets spanned several decades and included a broad range of variables such as country-level prevalence of mental disorders (depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and eating disorders), population coverage indicators, and symptom-based survey results from the U.S.
The selection criteria for these datasets were grounded in data quality, consistency, and granularity. Datasets with missing identifiers, insufficient temporal coverage, or ambiguous metrics were excluded to preserve analytical rigor. The chosen datasets were then merged and aligned using common attributes like country codes, year, and disorder type. This harmonization enabled cross-comparative analysis across geographic and temporal dimensions.
Preprocessing involved several key stages. First, null values were handled using domain-specific imputation strategies or excluded when data integrity could not be maintained. Data types were standardized, and categorical variables such as region and disorder type were encoded for compatibility with machine learning models. Skewed distributions were normalized using log transformations, and outliers were identified and mitigated using interquartile range (IQR) and z-score techniques.
To ensure feature integrity, exploratory data analysis (EDA) was conducted to assess multicollinearity and feature relevance. Visualizations such as boxplots, histograms, and heatmaps helped validate assumptions and informed the selection of input variables. In the final stage, the dataset was split into training and testing subsets, stratified by region and year to ensure balanced representation. This meticulous preprocessing pipeline established a strong foundation for reliable modeling and reproducible insights.
Critical Analysis

A thorough evaluation of the AI model’s performance reveals strong predictive capabilities and reliable generalization, indicative of its suitability for real-world deployment in healthcare analytics. The RandomForestRegressor achieved an R² score exceeding 0.85 across multiple validation folds, confirming its ability to capture the underlying relationships between input features (mental disorder prevalence rates) and the target outcome (eating disorder prevalence). The low MAE and RMSE values across the test datasets underscored the model’s robustness and minimized prediction variance.
One of the strengths of this model is its resilience to overfitting, particularly due to the ensemble nature of Random Forests. Feature importance plots consistently highlighted depressive and anxiety disorders as dominant predictors, which aligns well with existing psychiatric research and validates the model’s interpretability. This transparency in prediction logic strengthens the trustworthiness of the AI model for clinical or policy-related applications.
Moreover, performance diagnostics extended beyond basic accuracy metrics. Residual plots showed a balanced distribution of errors, confirming that the model does not exhibit systemic bias toward particular regions or time periods. Cross-validation using K-Fold techniques further enhanced confidence in generalization, as performance remained consistent across various subsets of data.
The model also demonstrated adaptability under diverse data conditions. When trained with subsets excluding high-income countries, it still retained strong performance highlighting its potential for global application, especially in data-scarce regions. While it is acknowledged that limited data availability and underreporting can impact predictions, sensitivity analysis and simulation runs helped quantify the range of variability, thereby equipping users with a clearer understanding of forecast uncertainty.
In summary, the model's comprehensive evaluation illustrates a high degree of reliability, relevance, and operational feasibility. It not only delivers accurate forecasts but also promotes explainable AI practices in a high-stakes domain, offering a technically sound and ethically aligned tool for enhancing global mental health strategies.

The technical implementation of this project exemplifies a strong synergy between advanced AI methodology and practical business relevance. Bias Detection and Evaluation

Given the complexity of healthcare data, the project acknowledged and addressed multiple biases that could compromise model reliability and fairness. One of the most critical concerns was data coverage bias, where underrepresented regions such as Sub-Saharan Africa or South Asia had insufficient historical data points. This skewed the model’s learning process, as regions with richer datasets (e.g., North America, Western Europe) naturally received greater model accuracy.
Another significant issue was diagnosis bias differences in healthcare infrastructure and mental health awareness led to unequal rates of disorder detection and reporting. A country with low reported prevalence might reflect a lack of diagnosis mechanisms rather than actual lower incidence. Additionally, temporal bias emerged due to inconsistencies in data availability over time, with recent years typically better represented and standardized.
To mitigate these effects, the team applied preprocessing filters to remove extreme outliers and highly sparse data entries. Log-transformation helped address skewed distributions, while imputation techniques filled smaller data gaps. We also annotated visual outputs to reflect confidence variability, explicitly stating when data may not fully capture reality. Although some bias is intrinsic to global health data, these steps contributed to fairer and more transparent model outcomes.

Challenges

Throughout the project, the team encountered several obstacles, particularly in working with globally sourced, heterogeneous health data. The quality and consistency of data posed the first major challenge, as datasets varied widely in formatting, terminology, and completeness. It required intensive data cleaning, normalization, and integration routines to prepare for analysis and modeling.
From a performance standpoint, maintaining computational efficiency was crucial. Running complex visualizations and machine learning models in real-time introduced latency issues, especially during dashboard interactions. This was addressed through code optimization, caching, and batch processing where possible. Another persistent issue was model interpretability. While Random Forests offer decent transparency, explaining predictions to non-technical users remained a priority, prompting the addition of contextual annotations and educational tooltips within the dashboard.
Bias mitigation  emerged as both a technical and ethical challenge. Deciding which regions to include, how to handle underreporting, and how to represent uncertainty required nuanced judgment. Finally, UI responsiveness and user experience became focal points, as the dashboard had to function seamlessly across devices while adapting to user input and varying dataset sizes. These cumulative challenges were met through iterative development, cross-validation, and user testing, shaping a more resilient final product.
